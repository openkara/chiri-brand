## Post B: Standalone — The New Haves and Have-Nots
**Topic:** The AI divide is no longer about who has AI. It is about who has access to the best AI.
**Hook:** Under 210 characters, earns the "see more."

---

We talk a lot about the AI haves and have-nots.

Usually we mean: companies that have adopted AI versus companies that haven't.

That divide is real. But a second divide is forming underneath it.

It is the divide between companies with access to frontier AI — the most capable, most current models — and companies that get pushed into weaker, more limited tiers.

Compute is scarce. Security restrictions are tightening. The most powerful models are being rationed to a select few partners. And the gap between frontier capabilities and "good enough" models is not shrinking. It is widening.

The companies that planned to compete on broadly equal AI access are discovering that the playing field was never going to stay level.

This is not a reason to panic. It is a reason to think carefully about your AI infrastructure now, before access becomes a competitive moat that is impossible to close.

The companies that build a governed, model-agnostic AI layer today are the ones that can upgrade to whatever model is best tomorrow. The ones that don't are betting on a level playing field that may not exist.

What is your organization's plan if the model you depend on becomes restricted or repriced?

## Post C: Standalone — Model-Agnostic is Not a Feature, It's a Strategy
**Topic:** The companies that win won't be locked into any single model.
**Hook:** Under 210 characters, earns the "see more."

---

"Model-agnostic" used to sound like a nice-to-have.

It is starting to sound like a survival strategy.

Anthropic's Mythos model was released only to a handful of U.S.-based partners. OpenAI followed the same pattern. Compute is scarce. Security restrictions are tightening. Frontier model access is becoming selective — and the companies inside the inner circle are not the ones most people are building their AI strategy around.

The companies that built their AI infrastructure around a single model are discovering what vendor lock-in looks like in a world where access can be restricted, repriced, or revoked.

The companies that built a model-agnostic layer — one that can route to whatever model is best for a given task, from whatever provider is available — are in a fundamentally different position.

This is why Chiri is built the way it is. Not because we have a preference for flexibility. Because we have been watching this market long enough to know that the model you depend on today may not be the model you have access to tomorrow.

The infrastructure underneath your AI matters more than the model on top of it.

What would happen to your AI workflows if your primary model became unavailable tomorrow?
