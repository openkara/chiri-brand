## Matthew Wimberly's Key Speaking Moments

Matthew speaks at three main points in the panel. All timestamps are from the original video file.

### Speaking Moment 1: "I am a recovering 10x engineer" — ROI and Use Case Selection
**Timestamps: 13:35 — 15:20 (approx 1m45s)**

Matthew is asked what ROI means in the context of AI. His answer is the most grounded and specific of the panel:

> "I'm going to start by just taking a step back and saying we hear 10x a lot. It's on our website. I'm guilty of this. We say 10x everyone. I am a recovering 10x engineer."

> "When we talk about identifying areas for ROI... some of the clients that we interact with, we knew one where I think they spent about five hours a day roughly making presentations. Like their solutions engineer team, they're just churning out all of these presentation templates one after one. We brought that down to, I think, under five minutes."

> "What we're trying to do is identify measurables initially. That's the beachhead getting in. And then, of course, those use cases expand from that."

**Key insight:** The ROI conversation should start with measurable, specific use cases — not 10x promises. The beachhead is one workflow, one measurable win.

---

### Speaking Moment 2: "A system of action" — Data and AI Architecture
**Timestamps: 24:38 — 26:00 (approx 1m22s)**

Matthew makes a sharp, specific architectural point about how AI sits above systems of record and systems of engagement:

> "Typically an organization only moves as fast as their slowest system, and that is typically the system of record. Systems of engagement are made to be adapted. They're made to be changed often. And so the beauty of AI is that it sits over top of both of those. And so that — I coin or typically use the term a system of action. It can reach into both."

> "Leave your data, your oldest data where it is, get your heuristics about what's there, map the roles and permission requirements, and literally let your Salesforce interface rot and melt away and only interact with it via AI."

> "Because then you are abstracting away a significant amount of the pain points that the business is having, which is I have too many interfaces that I need to go through. I live in 15 different tabs and there's like context silos."

**Key insight:** AI is a "system of action" — it sits above both systems of record and systems of engagement. You don't need to move your data. You need to let AI reach into it.

---

### Speaking Moment 3: "Contain your blast radius" — Agentic AI and Risk
**Timestamps: 26:34 — 27:39 (approx 1m05s)**

Matthew gives a practical, specific answer on how to manage agentic AI risk:

> "Separate read-only and read-write and contain a blast radius around anything that has a negative effect. If you're worried about this agent is going to send an email and I'm not going to approve it, then have it make a draft."

> "I think when you approach a customer and you find a really good use case, then you just kind of understand what they do manually today and then what parts can be automated with a human in the loop, and then start with everything as a human in the loop and then eventually make that autonomous."

> "Contain your blast radius based on what your customer is willing to do."

**Key insight:** Start with human-in-the-loop. Define the blast radius by what your customer is willing to accept. Don't let legal freak out — start with drafts, not sends.
